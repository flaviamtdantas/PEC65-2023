Authored by Flavia Dantas (SUNY Cortland) & Pedro Paulo Zahluth Bastos (UNICAMP) · Endorsed by 51 signatories

We hereby publicly express our formal and emphatic repudiation of Proposed Constitutional Amendment No. 65 of 2023 (PEC 65/2023), authored by Senator Vanderlan Cardoso (PSD-GO), currently under consideration in the Constitution and Justice Committee (CCJ) of the Federal Senate, with a substitute bill presented by Rapporteur Senator Plínio Valério (PSDB-AM) on April 16, 2026.

PEC 65/2023 proposes to grant the Central Bank of Brazil (BCB) broad financial, budgetary, and administrative autonomy, removing it from the Annual Budget Law (LOA) so that it may finance itself with seigniorage revenue — without parliamentary approval — and without establishing proportional mechanisms of accountability and democratic control. In doing so, it reduces the capacity for effective oversight by Congress, by the Federal Court of Accounts (TCU),1 and by society over decisions of broad macroeconomic impact. Presented as a technical modernization, the proposal creates a dangerous vacuum of democratic accountability over the country's principal monetary institution and renders the BCB more vulnerable to political pressure, to lobbyists, and to the financial sector with respect to the appointment of directors and the composition of its staff.

Moreover, it withdraws from the Treasury and from the public accounts a revenue that rightfully belongs to them, thereby increasing the General Government Gross Debt (DBGG): seigniorage. Under the amendment, seigniorage would no longer reduce the debt, because it would be treated as the Central Bank's "own revenue," with no obligation to transfer it to the Brazilian State. Its value amounted to R$ 210 billion between 2017 and 2025 — R$ 23.3 billion per year — while the BCB's administrative expenditure is R$ 4.8 billion per year. In other words, seigniorage is roughly five times what the Bank spends to operate. The amendment opens legal space for the patrimonialist appropriation of seigniorage and grants the Central Bank enormous fiscal latitude, in stark contrast with the cuts to social spending demanded by the financial market and by the BCB's own president.

We formally repudiate PEC 65/2023 and propose

  1. Full rejection of PEC 65/2023 by the Senate's Constitution and Justice Committee (CCJ);
  2. Preservation of the current model of transferring seigniorage revenue to the National Treasury;
  3. Maintenance of full parliamentary oversight over the BCB's budget and of the TCU's complete jurisdiction over its accounts;
  4. The opening of a Senate investigation into the drafting of the amendments to PEC 65/2023, especially Amendment 11, in light of Operation Compliance Zero;
  5. The strengthening of the BCB's accountability mechanisms before Congress, including (I) improving its subjection to the budget cycle and (II) regulating the grounds for holding BCB directors accountable for insufficient performance, as referred to in Art. 5, IV of Complementary Law 179/2021;
  6. The opening of a public debate on the mechanisms through which the BCB answers to society in its role as regulator and supervisor of the banking system.
I

Whose Interests Does PEC 65/2023 Serve? The Financial Sector and the Capture of the State

The amendment creates "selective independence": it removes the BCB from the democratic control of the State (Congress, the TCU, the Executive), while keeping it structurally porous to the influence of the financial market. The checks of the constituted powers are lost — but the private sector's channels of access remain open.

The legislative history of PEC 65/2023 reveals clearly who is driving and who benefits from the proposal: the private financial system. The case of Amendment 11 (the "Master" Amendment) by Senator Ciro Nogueira — which proposed raising the deposit-insurance guarantee (FGC) from R$ 250,000 to R$ 1 million, directly benefiting large depositors of Banco Master — is under investigation by the Federal Police in Operation Compliance Zero, on suspicion that the amendment was drafted directly by a financial institution.

A Central Bank that is budgetarily autonomous and detached from parliamentary oversight becomes, structurally, a regulator more vulnerable to pressure from the very sector it regulates. Paragraphs 8 and 9 of the new Art. 164 in the substitute bill expand the mechanisms for emergency financial assistance; although § 9 defers its regulation to complementary legislation, these powers would be exercised by a Central Bank that the amendment removes from ordinary budgetary and parliamentary control. Article 8, in turn, concentrates exclusive authority over PIX — the payment infrastructure of more than 170 million Brazilians — in the BCB. Although it prohibits its privatization, the amendment offers no countervailing democratic scrutiny proportional to the importance of this infrastructure, leaving its governance in the hands of an institution deliberately removed from budgetary and parliamentary control. Taken together, these provisions construct an insulated regulator that is difficult to hold accountable — precisely the environment that the private financial sector seeks in order to operate with less scrutiny.

II

The Fallacy of "Independence": Around the World, Central Banks Answer to the Law and to Parliament

The amendment invokes "alignment with international best practices" as its central justification. This claim is factually incorrect. None of the world's major central banks possesses the combination of financial autonomy, budgetary exemption, and parliamentary shielding that the amendment seeks to create. In every relevant case, there is a balance between operational financial autonomy and budgetary discipline:

  • United States — Federal Reserve (Fed): subject to the Federal Reserve Act; the Chair testifies before Congress twice a year (Humphrey-Hawkins Act); audited by the GAO (Government Accountability Office, the oversight arm of Congress) and by its own Inspector General; Congress may amend or abolish the Fed by ordinary legislation. Surplus over operating expenses is transferred to the Treasury through an automatic legal mechanism, with no discretion on the part of the Fed's board.
  • United Kingdom — Bank of England: the inflation target is set by the Treasury; the Governor is accountable to Parliament's Treasury Committee; the government may issue directives in "extreme circumstances" (Bank of England Act, 1998). The Bank is part of the Treasury Single Account and is therefore subject to the budget cycle.
  • Euro Area — ECB: subject to the EU Treaty; the President appears quarterly before the European Parliament; the Parliament may request audits.
  • Japan — Bank of Japan: its directors are appointed with the approval of the Diet; it reports to parliament every six months; it issues a joint statement of objectives with the government (2013). Net profit is transferred to the Treasury after deduction of legal reserves defined by the Ministry of Finance.

The historical trend among major central banks is toward public ownership: Bank of England (1946), Bundesbank (1957), Banque de France (1945).2 In none of these countries is seigniorage revenue excluded from the State's fiscal accounts. In all of them, operational autonomy is inseparable from a legal obligation to remit profits to the Treasury — by legal mandate, not at the bank's discretion. PEC 65/2023 offers no equivalent guarantee: it delegates the financial relationship between the BCB and the Union to future ordinary legislation (Art. 164, § 7), deferring to a law that does not yet exist the question of whether and how much of its results would be returned to the Treasury — without the legal remittance mandate that today ties seigniorage to the reduction of the public debt. Complementary Law No. 179/2021 already guarantees the BCB operational autonomy. PEC 65/2023 goes well beyond any comparable benchmark.

III

The Democratic Vacuum: To Whom Would the BCB Answer?

Today, the BCB is subject to multiple mechanisms of oversight and accountability: a budget approved by Congress in the LOA; financial statements audited by the TCU; directors summoned to testify before parliamentary committees; regulatory decisions subject to judicial review. The amendment systematically dismantles these mechanisms — it removes the BCB from the LOA, weakens the TCU's jurisdiction, and, through Amendment 14 by Senator Sérgio Moro (partially accepted), grants it the prerogative to submit legislative proposals directly to the President of the Republic — an unprecedented form of legislative initiative among central banks.

The central question the amendment fails to answer is: to whom would the BCB have to account? Who would guarantee that the BCB is in fact investigating and regulating the banking sector rigorously and impartially, if it is itself financially dependent on the health of that sector? Seigniorage grows with the monetary base and with the level of the interest rate; revenue from reserves grows with the size of the financial system; the BCB approves, supervises, and may bail out the very institutions that, indirectly, feed its budget. This is the classic definition of a captured regulator: an agent designed to serve the public, but whose incentives systematically align it with the interests of the regulated.

The Banco Master case shows why keeping the Central Bank under democratic control — with congressional authorization, full TCU jurisdiction, and reporting in the LOA — matters. Between 2024 and 2025, BRB — a public bank — acquired more than R$ 30 billion in Banco Master loan portfolios, some of them fraudulent or of poor quality. The adequacy of the BCB's supervision of these operations is currently under investigation by the Public Prosecutor's Office before the TCU. It is precisely the TCU, Congress, and public oversight — which the amendment would weaken — that today make it possible to scrutinize supervisory failures. Under PEC 65/2023, with the BCB outside the LOA and the TCU's jurisdiction reduced, such scrutiny would be far more difficult. Who watches the watchman?

IV

Perverse Incentives: The BCB as Both Judge and Party

The amendment's self-financing structure creates incentives that contradict the BCB's public mandate. By allowing the Central Bank to retain seigniorage revenue — determined by the relationship between the monetary base and the interest rate, and which grows with high inflation and high interest rates — the proposal ties the BCB's own budget to variables it directly influences, such as the interest rate, the liquidity of the financial system, and the scale of its monetary liabilities. The BCB would thus come to benefit institutionally from macroeconomic conditions it is supposed to manage in the public interest, rather than for its own budget. And because seigniorage depends on interest rates, the BCB would acquire a conflict of incentives regarding the maintenance of high interest rates.

More seriously: a self-financing BCB has a structural incentive to be a more lenient regulator. Looser regulation → a larger financial sector, more credit and financial operations → greater demand for the BCB's monetary liabilities → more seigniorage → a larger own budget for the BCB.

Between 2017 and 2025, seigniorage in the strict sense — the nominal interest on the monetary base — totaled R$ 210 billion in December 2025 values, about five times the BCB's administrative expenditure over the period.3 Under the current regime, the Bank's positive results are legally earmarked for reducing the federal securities debt; the amendment would allow this revenue — on the order of five times its administrative expenditure — to be retained by the BCB, with no legal basis for the Treasury to demand its return.

V

Constitutionality and Social Rights

The proposal may violate Art. 60, § 4, IV of the 1988 Constitution, which protects individual rights and guarantees as an entrenched clause.4 By transferring seigniorage revenue currently used to reduce the gross public debt, the amendment compromises the State's capacity to honor constitutionally guaranteed social rights. Research published by the World Bank (Aklin, Kern & Negre, 2021) finds an association between greater central bank independence and rising income inequality.5

The independence of a central bank is not an end in itself — it is a means to ensure monetary stability for the benefit of the population. It is legitimate only when joined to democratic accountability, transparency, and the constitutional objectives of the State. PEC 65/2023 secures none of these — and, in failing to do so, places the State at the service of the financial sector rather than of society.

Brasília — June 3, 2026
